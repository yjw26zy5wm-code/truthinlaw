Understanding the Problem

AI doesn't look up the law. It guesses what the law sounds like.

When you ask ChatGPT, Google, or another AI tool about a legal question, it doesn't search a real legal database. It predicts what a legally-sounding answer should look like — based on patterns in its training data.

Sometimes it's right. Sometimes it invents court cases that don't exist, quotes judges who never said those words, or cites laws that were repealed years ago.

This is called a hallucination — and it happens even with tools specifically designed for law.

What does a hallucination look like?

Imagine asking an AI: "What are my rights as a tenant if my landlord won't fix the heat?" The AI responds confidently — citing a specific court case, a statute number, even a judge's name. It looks real. It sounds authoritative. But the case never happened. The statute number is wrong. The quote was never said. You act on it. And now you're in worse trouble than before.

The hard truth: Even expensive legal AI tools used by actual lawyers have been caught doing this — resulting in court sanctions, dismissed cases, and thousands of dollars in fines. If it happens to trained attorneys, it can absolutely happen to you.

Protect Yourself

Warning signs that an AI legal answer might be wrong

You don't need a law degree to spot these red flags. If any of these apply to the AI response you received, stop and verify before acting on it.

!

The AI cited a specific case name and number

Case citations like "Smith v. Jones, 442 U.S. 735 (1979)" sound official — but AI frequently invents them. Before trusting any cited case, search for it on Google Scholar or CourtListener (both free).

!

The AI quoted a judge or statute directly

Quotation marks feel authoritative. But AI often generates plausible-sounding quotes that were never actually said or written. Always find the original document to verify any quoted language.

!

The AI answered with complete confidence and no caveats

Real legal answers are almost always "it depends." If an AI gives you a definitive yes or no with no uncertainty, that confidence is a warning sign — not reassurance.

!

The AI referenced a specific law or statute number

Statute numbers like "42 U.S.C. § 1983" are easy to fabricate. Laws also get amended or repealed. Always verify the statute exists and is current at your state legislature's official website or on congress.gov.

!

The stakes are high — housing, custody, immigration, debt

AI is fine for general information. But if you're facing eviction, a custody dispute, an immigration decision, or serious debt — do not act on AI advice alone. The cost of being wrong is too high.

!

You can't find the case anywhere online

If you search for a case the AI cited and can't find it on Google, Google Scholar, or CourtListener — it probably doesn't exist. Trust that instinct. The absence of a case is a clear sign of hallucination.

What To Do Instead

How to actually verify a legal claim in 5 steps

You don't need a lawyer to do basic verification. Here's a simple process anyone can follow.

Write down the specific claim

Identify exactly what the AI told you — the case name, statute number, or legal rule. Be specific.

Search CourtListener or Google Scholar

Both are free. Search the exact case name. If it doesn't appear, it may not exist.

Read the original, not the summary

Find the actual case text or statute. Does it actually say what the AI claimed? Often it doesn't.

Check if the law is still current

Laws change. Check your state legislature's website or congress.gov to confirm the statute hasn't been amended or repealed.

When in doubt, find free legal help

If the stakes are high, contact a legal aid organization. Many are free for people who qualify. See our resources page.
